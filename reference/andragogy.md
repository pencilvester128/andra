# andragogy.md — the six principles as review criteria

Malcolm Knowles' andragogy is the durable frame for judging whether a training plan
will land with adult professionals. These six principles are Andra's primary diagnostic
lens (`rules.md` RULE-05). Cited, not restated, by every andragogy flag. Source:
`research/andragogy-research.md` Part II; personal-experience grounding in
`research/andragogy-personal-experience.md`.

Each principle below carries a **what-good looks like** / **what-bad looks like** pair
so a flag can point at the concrete textual signal, not a vibe.

---

## 1. Need to Know

**The principle:** professionals resist training framed as "the company wants you to
use this." They engage when training answers *"how will this make my specific role
easier, faster, or better"* — proven with their own real work products, not a generic
demo.

- **What-good looks like:** the plan states, in the first few minutes of content, the
  specific task/output this changes for this specific role — named, not implied.
- **What-bad looks like ("the Curse of Mastery"):** the plan opens with what the tool
  *is* or *can do* (features, capabilities, a tour) rather than what changes for the
  learner. See `heuristics.md § Curse of Mastery` — this is the single most common
  first-draft failure and worth checking first.
- **Flag test:** can you find, in the first page/slide of the plan, a sentence that
  names *this audience's* task and *this audience's* payoff? If not, that's a Principle-1
  flag regardless of how good the rest of the content is.

## 2. Self-Concept and Autonomy

**The principle:** experienced professionals are not blank-slate students. Effective
upskilling positions the learner as a domain expert *adding* a capability, not a novice
*starting over*.

- **What-good looks like:** language that explicitly anchors the new skill to the
  learner's existing judgment — "you already know how to spot a bad client brief; this
  just gets you there faster."
- **What-bad looks like:** any framing, overt or inferred, that implies the learner's
  domain expertise doesn't count here, or that the training starts from zero. Also watch
  the exercise design itself, not just the prose — an exercise that requires no prior
  judgment to complete is a Principle-2 problem even if the language is respectful.
- **Flag test:** read every sentence that talks about who the learner is. Does any of
  them, even by omission, treat the learner as a novice rather than an expert acquiring
  a layer? See `heuristics.md § Sage-on-the-Stage Default` — the sneaky version of this
  failure comes from the presenter's own mindset, not a deliberate insult.

## 3. Prior Experience as a Resource

**The principle:** the professional's years of domain expertise are the raw material AI
operates on. Training should connect new AI capability to the learner's existing pattern
recognition and quality standards — not present AI as a replacement for that judgment.

- **What-good looks like:** exercises that ask the learner to *evaluate* or *curate* an
  AI output using judgment they already have, rather than just execute a prompt.
- **What-bad looks like:** framing AI output as ready-to-use rather than as raw material
  the learner's judgment still gates. This failure compounds with Principle-7
  (augmentation-not-replacement, see `resistance.md`) — flag both if both are present,
  but they are distinct mechanisms: this one is about *pedagogical design*, that one is
  about *psychological framing*.
- **Flag test:** in the practice exercises, does the learner's own expertise do any
  visible work, or does the exercise treat the AI's first output as the finish line?

## 4. Readiness to Learn

**The principle:** timing upskilling to a real, current pain point — a task the learner
does weekly and finds tedious — produces far higher engagement than generic "AI
fundamentals" disconnected from daily friction.

- **What-good looks like:** the plan names a specific, recurring, currently-painful task
  and frames the session around solving it.
- **What-bad looks like:** vague, generic framing ("AI can help you be more productive")
  with no specific recurring task named. Vagueness doesn't just fail to help — it
  actively *erodes* readiness, because it reads as not-actually-applicable and the
  learner disengages.
- **Flag test:** could you swap this plan's stated "pain point" into a plan for a
  different team with zero edits? If yes, it isn't specific enough to be a real pain
  point — it's a placeholder. Push the author to name the actual task.

## 5. Problem-Centered, Not Subject-Centered

**The principle:** training must organize around the professional's actual recurring
tasks and workflows, not abstract concepts about the technology.

- **What-good looks like:** the session is structured as "here's your task, here's how
  the tool changes it," with theory introduced only as much as is needed to support that.
- **What-bad looks like:** the plan is organized by *topic* (what the tool does, feature
  by feature) rather than by *task*. This is the default first-draft shape for anyone
  who is a subject-matter expert and not a trainer — expect to find it, and treat finding
  it as normal, not as a sign the author did something wrong.
- **Flag test:** read the plan's own outline / table of contents / slide titles. Are they
  named after tool features or after the learner's tasks? A subject-centered outline is
  visible at the structural level before you even read the content.
- **Exception, not a violation:** some content genuinely has to be delivered as theory
  (a concept the task depends on). That's fine — the check is on *ratio and framing*, not
  a zero-theory rule. Don't flag a plan for including necessary theory; flag it for
  organizing the *whole session* around theory instead of tasks.

## 6. Intrinsic Motivation

**The principle:** the strongest long-term driver of adoption is the learner's own
experience of mastery and improved output — not a compliance mandate. Design should
engineer early "wow" moments where the learner sees direct proof the tool improved
their own work.

- **What-good looks like:** at least one moment, early in the session, where the learner
  produces something with the tool using their *own* real material and sees a
  concrete improvement or time saved.
- **What-bad looks like:** either (a) no engineered early win anywhere in the plan, or
  (b) *too many* forced "wow" moments that read as manufactured rather than genuine —
  both degrade intrinsic motivation, for different reasons. See
  `heuristics.md § Engineer Early Wins`.
- **Flag test:** find the first moment in the plan where the learner does something
  hands-on with their own material. How far into the session is it, and does the plan
  describe what they should see change? If it's absent, or it's demo-material instead of
  the learner's own work, that's a Principle-6 flag.

---

## How these six interact with the rollout arc

These are content-and-design criteria — they judge what's *in* a session. They are
distinct from, but feed, the rollout-arc criteria in `change-management.md` (sequencing,
follow-up, reinforcement) and the resistance criteria in `resistance.md` (framing,
psychological safety). A plan can score well on all six andragogy principles within a
single session and still fail because there's no follow-up structure — that's a
`change-management.md` flag, not an andragogy one. Keep the passes distinct so the
feedback names the *right* mechanism (RULE-08 prioritization depends on this).
