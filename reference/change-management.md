# change-management.md — the rollout arc

Andragogy (`andragogy.md`) judges a single session's content. This file judges the
**arc** — how sessions sequence, whether practice gets time to stick, and whether the
change survives past week one. Source: `research/andragogy-research.md` Parts III–IV,
VII; personal grounding in `research/andragogy-personal-experience.md`. Cited by
`rules.md` RULE-06.

**Snapshot flag (IDENT-07):** the statistics below are dated **2026-07**. The
frameworks (ADKAR, Lewin, the sequencing logic) are durable; the percentages are
perishable — state them as snapshot-dated, never as guaranteed-current, per RULE-12.

---

## ADKAR — the individual's path through the change

Five stages a *person*, not an org, must pass through. A rollout plan should show
awareness of all five, even if not labeled as such:

- **Awareness** — why this change, why now. Missing awareness reads as a mandate
  dropped from above with no rationale.
- **Desire** — personal motivation to participate. This is where andragogy Principles
  1 and 6 (need-to-know, intrinsic motivation) do the actual work — Desire is not
  manufactured by ADKAR itself, it's earned by the session design.
- **Knowledge** — how to actually use the tool. The content layer.
- **Ability** — demonstrated capability in real work, not just in a practice exercise. This is
  where the practice-loop design (`andragogy.md` Principle 6, the early-win check)
  either transfers or doesn't.
- **Reinforcement** — sustaining the change so it doesn't erode back to old habits.
  **This is the single most commonly missing stage in first-draft plans** — see
  "The follow-up gap" below.

## Lewin — Unfreeze / Change / Refreeze

A complementary organizational-level lens:

- **Unfreeze** — surfacing the cost of the status quo, building openness before content
  starts. A plan that jumps straight to "here's the tool" without any unfreeze read as
  presumptuous — it assumes buy-in that hasn't been earned yet.
- **Change** — the structured guidance during the transition itself (the session
  content).
- **Refreeze** — institutionalizing the new behavior: updated process, recognition,
  removing the old-process fallback. A plan with no refreeze step lets the change erode
  the moment attention moves elsewhere.

## The follow-up gap (the load-bearing check for this file)

The evidence is unambiguous on one point: **facilitated, cohort-based programs run
roughly 40% training effectiveness versus ~13% for self-paced, generic programs** — a
nearly threefold gap (InStride, 2026-07 snapshot). The mechanism behind that gap is
follow-up, not delivery format. A single "sit and get" with no scheduled return to the
material essentially guarantees the ADKAR Reinforcement stage never happens.

**What to check in a plan:**
- Is there an explicit second touchpoint scheduled — not "we'll check in sometime," but
  a named session or date, a week or two out from the first?
- Does that second touchpoint have a **different job** than the first — application and
  troubleshooting, not re-teaching content? A follow-up that just repeats the first
  session isn't reinforcement, it's a rerun.
- Is the gap between initial content and applied follow-up roughly a week? This maps to
  the spaced-practice / forgetting-curve research — see `heuristics.md § Forgetting-Curve
  Follow-up` — too short and there's been no real-world attempt to discuss; too long and
  the content has decayed before it's used.

**A plan with zero follow-up structure is not a minor gap — it is very likely the single
highest-leverage flag to raise**, because no amount of first-session polish survives an
organization's normal reversion to old habits without it (RULE-08 prioritization should
weight this heavily when present).

## Manager / champion involvement

Mid-level leaders are the natural reinforcement layer — they already field weekly
tool questions from their teams even without formal training in place. A rollout plan
that relies entirely on the single trainer/presenter for all follow-up, with no
named champions or manager involvement in sustaining the change, is under-resourced for
Reinforcement even if the initial session is excellent. Flag this as a resourcing gap,
distinct from the follow-up-scheduling gap above — a plan can have both, either, or
neither.

## Sector/function customization

Generic, one-size-fits-all AI training stalls; successful rollouts target a single,
well-defined pain point with deep contextual customization to the actual team's
workflow. This is the arc-level version of andragogy Principle 4 (readiness/real pain
point) — if a plan's content is generic, check whether the *whole rollout*, not just
one session, was designed for this specific team or lifted from a generic template.
