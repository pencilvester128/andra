# resistance.md — adoption psychology and framing

Even a well-sequenced, andragogically sound plan can fail if it triggers resistance the
author didn't anticipate. This file covers *why* people resist and how framing either
defuses or inflames it. Source: `research/andragogy-research.md` Parts III, VI;
grounding in `research/andragogy-personal-experience.md`. Cited by `rules.md` RULE-07.

**Snapshot flag (IDENT-07):** adoption percentages below are dated 2026-07 — durable
mechanism, perishable number, per RULE-12.

---

## Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) — the two beliefs that predict adoption

- **Perceived Usefulness (PU)** — will this improve my job performance?
- **Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU)** — will using it be free of effort?

A plan needs to do real work on *both*. Usefulness alone doesn't convert if the tool
still looks effortful to pick up; ease-of-use alone doesn't convert if the payoff is
unclear. **Check specifically:** does the plan start with the *full* range of the tool's
capability (high perceived effort, PEOU killer), or with a simplified first use case,
template, or guided first attempt? Complexity-first openings are a common and avoidable
PEOU failure — flag them even when the content itself is accurate.

## Resistance is not irrationality — name the real source

Resistance to technological change is a near-universal, predictable phenomenon, not a
personality flaw in the resisters. The literature converges on four real sources:

1. **Fear of obsolescence** — will I still have a job/role that matters?
2. **Loss of competence identity** — I am good at what I do; does this make that
   moot?
3. **Uncertainty about expectations** — what, exactly, am I now supposed to do
   differently?
4. **Lack of psychological safety to experiment and fail** — can I try this badly,
   in front of people, without cost?

**Flag test:** does the plan address any of these four *explicitly*, or does it assume
goodwill and skip straight to tool mechanics? A plan that never engages with why
someone might resist is not neutral — it's leaving the resistance unaddressed, which
research shows doesn't self-resolve.

## Augmentation, not replacement — the single highest-leverage framing check

Job-displacement anxiety is the most-cited employee concern around AI adoption (~75% in
recent survey data), and anxious workforces show dramatically lower training
effectiveness than optimistic ones (roughly 15% vs 50%). This makes framing a
prerequisite training objective, not a nice-to-have opener.

**What-good looks like:** the plan explicitly maps which parts of the role the tool
absorbs versus which parts still require (or are elevated to) human judgment — named,
not implied.

**What-bad looks like:** any language, even accidental, that frames the tool as
replacing the *role* rather than absorbing *tasks within* the role. This is often
unintentional — an enthusiastic "this tool can do X for you now" reads to an anxious
listener as "you are no longer needed for X," even when the author meant it as a
time-saved pitch. Flag the specific sentence, not the author's intent — you can't verify
intent (this is the same camera-test discipline `editing-craft.md` applies to Andra's
own feedback).

## Expertise-respect — resistance and andragogy overlap here

`andragogy.md` Principle 2 (self-concept/autonomy) and this resistance check are related
but distinct: Principle 2 is about pedagogical design (does the exercise treat the
learner as an expert?); this check is about psychological framing (does the *language
around the change* threaten the learner's professional identity?). A plan can pass one
and fail the other. Flag them separately so the author can locate the actual mechanism.

## Age and generational dynamics — flag the pattern, never the demographic

Research on digital-age adaptation finds AI skill gaps and adoption hesitancy are more
pronounced for some segments of a workforce even when those same segments carry stronger
judgment-based skills — a real and well-documented pattern. **The instruction for
Andra's own language, and the check on the author's plan, is identical: never target a
specific age group or generation directly.** Instead:

- Check whether the plan acknowledges that *some* experienced team members may be more
  hesitant, and whether it frames the case for adoption around the *value AI adds to
  their established judgment* — not around closing a generational gap.
- Flag any language in the plan, however well-intentioned, that singles out an age
  cohort ("this is especially for our older team members who...") — reframe the ask
  instead as behavior/comfort-level based, which is both more respectful and more
  accurate to the actual variance within any age group.

## Skeptic-to-innovator — what "success" looks like in framing terms

The goal state described in the research is a workforce that moves from skeptic toward
innovator through their own accumulated small wins — not through mandate compliance.
When reviewing a plan's framing throughout, ask: does the language assume the audience
needs to be *convinced*, or does it assume the audience will convince *themselves*
once they see it work on their own material? The latter is what the evidence supports;
the former (persuasion-heavy, feature-heavy opening) tends to read as a sales pitch and
can itself trigger the skepticism it's trying to overcome.
