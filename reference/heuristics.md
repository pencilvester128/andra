# heuristics.md — named field heuristics

Seven named patterns distilled from 11+ years running L&D, teacher training, and change
management for teams of professional learners — not textbook andragogy, but what the
failure and the fix actually look like on the ground when you're the one holding the
whiteboard marker. Each entry: **the pattern**, **how it shows up in a draft plan**, and
**the flag to raise**. Cited by `rules.md` RULE-05/RULE-06; cross-linked from
`andragogy.md` and `change-management.md` where they sharpen a principle.

---

## The Curse of Mastery

**The pattern:** the better someone is at a thing, the harder it becomes for them to
remember what a non-expert actually needs to know first. Planning a session is where
this bites hardest — it's surprisingly easy to lose track of "what do they actually need
to know, in its simplest form" while drafting, even for someone who is normally careful
about their audience.

**How it shows up:** the plan opens with, or is dominated by, what the tool *is* and
*can do* — a capability tour — rather than the one thing this audience needs to walk
away doing differently. This is almost always unintentional, not a sign of a lazy
author.

**The flag:** name it plainly as this specific pattern — "this reads like the Curse of
Mastery: the opening covers what the tool does, but not what changes for this team's
Tuesday-morning task. What's the one thing they need to know first?" Naming the pattern,
not just the symptom, helps the author recognize it next time they plan something.

## The Sage-on-the-Stage Default

**The pattern:** anyone turnkeying their own mastery to a team, without training as a
trainer, defaults — not by design, just by instinct — toward a subject-based,
one-directional "sit and get" where the presenter spews information and the audience
has no active moment. This happens even to trained educators; it is the default gravity
of "I know this well and need to explain it," not a mark of a bad presenter.

**How it shows up:** long content blocks with no built-in pause, no question, no hands-
on moment; an agenda that's entirely presenter-time.

**The flag:** ask where in the session the audience does something, not just listens.
If the honest answer is "nowhere until the very end, if at all," that's the flag —
frame it as the natural default to watch for, not a personal failing.

## The 20-Minute Attention Ceiling

**The pattern:** in a live sit-and-get, roughly 20 minutes is the realistic ceiling
before you need to re-anchor the audience by showing them themselves in the material
again — their task, their stakes, their payoff.

**How it shows up:** a content block longer than ~20 minutes with no re-anchoring
moment — no check-in, no "does this match what you're dealing with," no pause for a
quick application.

**The flag:** count the unbroken content stretches. Any stretch past ~20 minutes without
a re-anchor to the audience's own work is a flag, regardless of how good the content
itself is — attention erosion isn't a content-quality problem, it's a structural one.

## The Time-Scarcity Tax

**The pattern:** every professional audience is operating under real time scarcity, and
this is the single most common, most quietly corrosive cause of low adoption — not a
lack of motivation or a lack of skill. It's the classic "work on the business vs. work
in the business" tension: learning something new is front-loaded effort for later
payoff, and time-scarce professionals default to the immediate-gratification, reactive
mode.

**How it shows up:** a plan that assumes the audience will find independent time to
practice, explore, or follow up, with no protected time built into the plan itself. Also
shows up as an unrealistic estimate of how much a busy professional will do
unprompted between sessions.

**The flag:** does the plan protect time for practice and follow-up *within* the
session or the rollout schedule, or does it assume the audience will find that time on
their own? Assuming free time is the single most common way a well-designed rollout
quietly fails at the adoption stage — flag it even when everything else about the plan
is strong.

## Sit-and-Get vs. Apply — the gap that kills transfer

**The pattern:** theory and rationale ("the why") land in one sitting; genuine skill
transfer requires a *separate* application session, ideally about a week later, where
the audience actually uses the thing on their own work. Conflating these into one
session, or leaving application entirely unscheduled, is the single most common
structural gap in a first-draft rollout plan (this is the mechanism behind
`change-management.md`'s "follow-up gap," named at the session-pair level).

**How it shows up:** a single 30–60 minute session that tries to deliver both the
concept *and* expects application to happen automatically afterward, with no scheduled
second touchpoint.

**The flag:** is there a distinct, scheduled application session — not just a mention of
"go try it" — roughly a week after the concept session? If the plan is a single
standalone session with no return, that's the flag, and it's usually the highest-
leverage one available.

## Engineer Early Wins — deliberately, not fraudulently

**The pattern:** the strongest driver of durable adoption is the learner's own
experience of mastery — a real, felt "this worked" moment, early. The craft is in
engineering *a few* genuine ones deliberately, using the learner's own material, not
manufacturing an artificial number of forced "wow" moments that read as staged. Two
wins that are real land better than five that feel engineered.

**How it shows up (missing):** no moment in the plan where the audience sees a direct,
concrete improvement on their *own* work.
**How it shows up (overdone):** a plan stuffed with contrived "amazing!" moments that
don't connect to genuine audience material — this degrades trust and intrinsic
motivation just as much as having none.

**The flag:** find the first hands-on moment using the *audience's own* material. Is
there one? Is there exactly the right number (a few, not none, not a forced parade)?

## Forgetting-Curve Follow-up

**The pattern:** spacing the "why/theory" session and the "apply it" session by roughly
a week can actually work *with* the forgetting curve rather than against it — the
retrieval effort at the follow-up session strengthens the memory, rather than the
content simply decaying unused. This is the practical, timing-level version of
ADKAR's Reinforcement stage.

**How it shows up:** either no follow-up at all, or a follow-up scheduled so far out
(a month-plus) that the content has fully decayed before the return visit, or so soon
(same day) that there's been no real-world attempt to discuss yet.

**The flag:** check the actual gap between initial content and the scheduled follow-up.
Roughly a week is the sweet spot the personal-experience and forgetting-curve research
both point to — flag gaps that are absent, too short to allow real application, or too
long to still be useful.
