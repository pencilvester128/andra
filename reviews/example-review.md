## Top flags

**1. The agenda is subject-centered, and the client-memo task never appears in it.**
Sections 1–2 spend 20 of the 45 minutes on what Claude is and how LLMs work before
anything task-specific shows up — a classic Curse of Mastery pattern: because you
understand the tool well, explaining the tool feels like the natural place to start, but
your team doesn't need that first. They need to see their own Friday task solved. Per
andragogy Principle 1 (Need to Know) and Principle 5 (Problem-Centered), what would it
look like to open with an actual client-memo example instead, and let "what is Claude"
show up only as much as is needed to explain what just happened?

**2. There's no moment where the team drafts a memo using their own real client
data.** Section 3's demo is described as you demonstrating on a generic example, not
them practicing on their own work. Per Principle 6 (Intrinsic Motivation) and the
Engineer-Early-Wins heuristic, the strongest driver of adoption is the learner's own
felt "that actually worked" moment on material that's theirs. Is there room, even in the
existing 45 minutes, to have each person run one real (anonymized) memo through it
before the session ends?

**3. There's no follow-up session, and this is very likely the highest-leverage gap in
the whole plan.** A single 45-minute session with no scheduled return essentially
guarantees the content doesn't survive contact with a busy week — the research is
unambiguous that facilitated programs with real follow-up run roughly three times the
effectiveness of one-and-done sessions (a 2026-07-snapshot figure; may have moved
since). What would it take to schedule a second, shorter session about a week out — not
re-teaching the tool, just looking at memos people actually drafted that week and
troubleshooting?

## Start here

If you only fix one thing before the session: schedule the follow-up (flag 3). It's the
one gap that undermines the value of the other two, regardless of how they turn out.
